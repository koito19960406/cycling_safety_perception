Abstract
Cycling is critical in transitioning to more sustainable transportation systems for shorter trips, including first-
and-last-mile links to transit. To support this transition, cities need to provide cyclists with environments
where they feel safe and comfortable. Thus, analyzing cyclists’ safety perceptions is critical for planners and
decision-makers to improve cycling uptake, as this is the main deterrent for individuals to cycle. If cyclists
perceive the network as unsafe, they will prefer different modes for their regular trips.
Yet, capturing and understanding how individuals perceive cycling risk is complex and often slow, with
researchers defaulting to traditional surveys and in-loco interviews. In this study, we tackle this problem
by understanding the perception of cycling safety from real-world images, together with imagery data,
mapping data, and data processing tools. We use an Explainable Boosting Machine (EBM), a glassbox
machine learning algorithm, to analyze the impact of image characteristics and other mapping information
(i.e., urban elements) on individuals’ perceptions. Insights are captured directly from street-view images
and the surrounding built environment to allow researchers to individually analyze non-linear impacts of
each characteristic on each cycling environment.
Our results show how this approach facilitates the continuous assessment of changing cycling environ-
ments and its use in efficiently assessing different locations with the growing number of openly available
street-view images across cities. In turn, this can help evaluate more effectively how road environment
features relate to cyclists’ risk perception on the road.

1. Introduction1
All over the world, cities are investing in cycling infrastructure and network expansion, leading to an2
increase in cycling numbers (Pucher and Buehler, 2017). Many aspects impact the adoption of the bicycle3
as a means of transportation. For one, cycling can improve health (Oja et al., 2011; G¨ otschi et al., 2016)4
and reduce greenhouse gas emissions and air pollutants (Mason et al., 2015; Neves and Brand, 2019). Yet,5
many factors deter people from cycling. From these, safety concerns (fears of being involved in an accident)6
are often considered a major deterrent to cycling (Parkin et al., 2007; Winters et al., 2012; Sanders, 2015;7
Aldred, 2016; Aziz et al., 2018; F´ elix et al., 2019).8

When people view cycling as risky or dangerous, they are likely to opt for alternative transportation9
modes instead of cycling (Chaurand and Delhomme, 2013). This poses a challenge to any city’s efforts to10
boost cycling rates. As such, it is crucial to comprehend the factors that contribute to or exacerbate these11
safety concerns. Past research has found, among others, that road infrastructure and cycling facilities impact12
individuals’ sense of safety (Chataway et al., 2014; Ng et al., 2017; Wang and Akar, 2018; G¨ otschi et al.,13
2018; von St¨ ulpnagel and Binnig, 2022; Ferreira et al., 2022). Thus far, research has traditionally employed14
on-site surveys, post-ride interviews, and conventional choice modeling tools to understand the impact of15
different urban elements on individuals’ perceptions. However, these methods frequently prove impractical16
due to their relatively high costs, require considerable manual labor to analyze and understand, and limited17
ability to capture non-linear effects of urban environment characteristics.18
This work presents a new framework to uncover insights into the urban context’s impact on individuals’19
perception of cycling safety. In essence, we are interested in answering “ What urban elements impact20
one’s perception of cycling safety?”. To answer this, we use an explainable boosting machine (EBM)21
(Lou et al., 2013) as a way to predict perceived safety scores of street-view images from urban context22
factors, such as the presence of cycle lanes, vegetation, urban furniture, and presence of cars. As a result,23
one can learn the impact of each contextual element as it varies and its impact on either increasing or24
decreasing the sense of safety. EBM is a powerful, intelligible, and glassbox machine learning model that25
can account for both non-linear effects and pairwise interaction between factors, enabling more in-depth26
constructs to be formulated on individuals’ perceptions of safety. Drawn from both image and mapping data,27
which include built environment elements (e.g., presence of cycle lanes, vegetation, bollards), dynamic road28
elements (e.g., presence of cars, other cyclists, pedestrians) and urban morphology (e.g., type of intersections,29
connectivity), we analyze the non-linear influence of different factors on subjective safety scores of urban30
cycling environments.31
In the next part of the paper, we outline related work in Section 2. Section 3 details our methodology,32
including a description of the data and modeling tool used. Then, we present our results in Section 4.33
Section 5 discusses this study’s results and limitations. Finally, Section 6 concludes the paper and draws34
lines of future research.35
2. Background36
Perceived or subjective cycling safety describes an individual’s personal perception of the risk of cycling37
accidents. As the most significant barrier to urban cycling (Sanders, 2015; F´ elix et al., 2019), it is crucial38
to comprehend the factors that increase or decrease this sense of risk. Understanding this is essential for39
creating environments where cyclists feel safe, which can, in turn, increase cycling uptake. Fortunately,40
considerable efforts have been carried out recently to understand individuals’ perceptions of safety better.41
These include different data collection approaches, new modeling strategies, and the creation of various42
indices to facilitate the comparison of different cycling environments.43
Data collection typically assumes the use of qualitative and quantitative surveys, in situ or post-riding44
interviews to collect data on individuals’ perceptions to identify elements that negatively arouse individuals45
(Sanders, 2015; Aldred and Woodcock, 2015). Newer approaches, led by new technologies and the availability46
of open data in cities, have explored how to collect data on individuals’ perceptions through wearable sensors47
(Zeile et al., 2016), cycling videos (Parkin et al., 2007), mental maps (Manton et al., 2016), brain-imaging48
data (Bogacz et al., 2021), virtual reality environments (von St¨ ulpnagel and Krukar, 2018), semi-realistic49
street-view-style images (von St¨ ulpnagel and Binnig, 2022), real-world street-view pictures (Costa et al.,50
2023), or gaze behavior via eye tracking devices (Schmidt and von St¨ ulpnagel, 2018). In addition, newer data51
sources, including crowdsourced data have also been used to understand cycling dynamics in urban settings52
(Nelson et al., 2021; Ito and Biljecki, 2021). However, despite the methodology used, most approaches53
usually require critical preparation and control over what stimuli are presented to individuals. This manual54
task of carefully calibrating what is shown and enquired to users often limits the scalability and complexity55
of environments explored. Ultimately, this hinders results’ repeatability and transferability.56
To uncover the effect of what elements impact cyclists’ and non-cyclists’ perceptions of safety, several57
strategies have been employed by researchers. These range from using qualitative methods (through in-58
2
terviews or focus groups) to quantitative methods (using principal component analysis or discrete choice59
modeling). In all, both approaches seek to uncover what factors impact one’s perception of cycling risk,60
including the influence of urban elements or socio-demographic factors. Effects from traffic (Sanders, 2015),61
interactions with drivers and compliance with road rules (Lawson et al., 2013; Graystone et al., 2022), road62
and cycling infrastructure (Chataway et al., 2014; Ng et al., 2017) have been shown to impact the sense of63
safety of individuals. In this work, we are interested in analyzing the impact of different cycling context64
factors, mainly related to the built environment, the road, and its users. This has been explored before,65
such as the impact of intersection density (Wang and Akar, 2018), bicycle lanes (Ng et al., 2017; Wang and66
Akar, 2018), roads hierarchies and number of lanes (Chataway et al., 2014), lack of cycling network connec-67
tivity (F´ elix et al., 2019) and cycling facilities (Useche et al., 2019), presence of median refuge island (Wang68
and Akar, 2018), or roundabouts typologies (Møller and Hels, 2008). Yet, most studies look at factors69
independently, singling out one factor and understanding its effect on individuals’ perceptions. While this70
reduces the chances for colinearity and allows researchers to focus on one single factor, urban environments71
are complex and heterogeneously composed of many elements altogether, and such factors cannot be singled72
out easily.73
Other approaches focus less on a specific factor in the road environment, but use perceptional indicators74
and metrics to investigate types of different infrastructure layouts, aiding urban planners and designers in75
comparing and analyzing cycling conditions. Widely recognized indices like the Bicycle Stress Level (Sorton76
and Walsh, 1994) and the Level of Traffic Stress (Mekuria et al., 2012; Furth, 2017) attempt to assess77
bikeability and perceived risk. More recent approaches have expanded this strategy, deriving a bikeability78
index (Ito and Biljecki, 2021) and a perceived safety score (Costa et al., 2023) from street-view image-79
extracted features using computer vision techniques, offering an automated and scalable way to assess80
environments. Yet, not only are these indices usually created using linear effects of characteristics, but81
most other typical research methods (e.g., ordinal logit or factor analysis) often use linear-in-parameters82
approaches. While this is frequently a way to simplify and extract meaningful insights into safety perceptions,83
its effects may not be entirely linear, and thus, understanding a factor’s effect may be over-simplified or not84
be entirely captured in its entirety.85
All in all, understanding and analyzing the perception of cycling safety is critical to promoting cycling86
uptake. Research on the topic is typically based on surveys to quantify the impact of different factors on87
cyclists’ perceptions. Planners and decision-makers can, in turn, use this knowledge to devise policies or88
urban changes to make cyclists feel safer while cycling through a city. However, this undertaking is often89
slow and requires considerable manual labor to prepare and analyze results. We consider that significant90
progress, both in terms of scalability and insight-attainability, can be made using a different approach to91
studying the perception of cycling safety.92

This section describes the data and methodology used in our work. We start by detailing the data94

modeling approach using explainable boosting machine to uncover non-linear effects of urban elements on96

3.1. Data98
In this work, we explore how we can extract insights into urban elements on individuals’ perceptions99
of cycling safety. For this, we make use of available perception of safety scores (PSS) from 3850 street-100
view images, where images portray real-world cycling environments from various locations across Berlin.101
Perceived scores for each image were previously obtained (Costa et al., 2023) and result from a survey102
aimed at ranking street-view images according to individuals’ perception of safety. In short, images were103
downloaded from Mapillary2, which consists of street-level images contributed collaboratively by users. The104

First, we begin by semantically segmenting images to extract relevant information about their composi-120
tion. Given a set of classes or objects, semantic segmentation is a computer vision task that categorizes each121
image’s pixels into a class or object. In essence, its goal is to produce a map of an image to its corresponding122
object. In our setting, we seek to retrieve information about urban and transport elements present in the123
image, such as roads, sidewalks, vegetation, cars, and bicycles, among others. For this, we process every im-124
age in our dataset using the publicly available Mask2Former (Cheng et al., 2022), trained using street-view125
images and able to detect 63 different urban elements. Figure 3 shows how semantic information is retrieved126
from images and Table 1 presents descriptive statistics on the object classes extracted from images. Using127
the extracted segmentation map, we then obtained objects’ areas on the image (e.g., what percentage of128
the image consists of cars, roads, pedestrians, and so forth for all available classes) and use this detailed129
information about urban environment composition as part of the regressors in our analysis.130
Second, to enrich the information we retrieved directly from images, we collect additional built environ-131
ment context data from available mapping data. For this, we used OpenStreetMap 3 (OSM), which contains132
geographically open mapping data contributed by users (e.g., road, sidewalks, cycling lanes, trees, build-133
ings). Since we are interested in the immediate surroundings of the presented images, we used each image’s134
location coordinates to extract all available information within a 25-meter radius, including the existence of,135
for example, road typologies, street furniture, shops, trees, or bollards. Figure 4 shows how data is extracted136
from OSM given an image location. We added the mapping data as we are interested in capturing more137
static physical urban element effects that cannot be automatically retrieved from images. For example, these138
include the existence and type of intersections, more detailed information on street furniture, and others.139
Moreover, using the transportation network we retrieved from OSM, we also computed urban typology met-140
rics, including centrality, betweenness, and closeness. In total, 192 urban characteristics are extracted for141
each environment (i.e., image location), which we also use as independent variables in our analysis.142

Because of its GAM form, EBM learns contributions from each feature and pairwise interactions between154
features in a modular format, allowing for easy assessment, understanding, and visualization of each feature’s155
contribution to the predicted score. In this sense, they work as a multiple variable linear regression, yet each156
regressor is automatically transformed via a lookup table per feature, improving intelligibility and prediction157
power. In turn, this allows us to check for non-linear impacts of regressors on the perceived safety score,158
enabling a more in-depth and non-linear analysis of individuals’ perceptions.159
In our setting, we use EBM as a way to predict the perceived safety scores of each street-view image160
using the aforementioned regressors (i.e., built environment elements drawn from semantic and mapping161
data), such as the presence of cycle lanes, vegetation, urban furniture, and presence of cars. Again, we162
explicitly employ such approach to be able to uncover non-linear and pairwise effects from these contextual163
elements and how they moderate individuals’ increased ou decreased sense of safety.164

4. Results165
This section details the results of modeling urban elements’ impact on cycling perception of safety using166
EBM. We present the results of both the effects of data extracted directly from the images and the features167
retrieved from mapping data, highlighting their impacts and providing insights into cycling perception of168
safety.169
We estimated an EBM using Python to program and evaluate the model and make the code publicly170
available4. For the results produced, EBM was trained using 15 maximum bins for each feature, a total of171
256 possible pairwise interactions, 0.1 as our learning rate, 8000 maximum number of rounds, 1 minimum172
sample per leaf, 25 maximum leaves, 16 inner bags, 8 outer bags, and 40 rounds for early stopping. Lastly,173
as aforementioned, we use the urban context data, which includes semantic and mapping data comprising a174
total of 250 variables, to model our perception safety scores.175
In the end, we achieved a model with R2 = 70.1% (RMSE = 4.04), effectively learning what and how176
various urban elements impact the perception of cycling safety. As previously mentioned, two major benefits177
of using EBM are understanding the non-linear effects of different urban elements and being able to perform178
large-scale unified analysis using a vast number of cycling context features. However, covering and describing179
all the characteristics and variables’ effects in this article is impossible as their number is too large. Hence,180
we highlight our main findings here, while the complete results can be found in the Supplementary Material.181
First, we take a look at the impact of semantic data. We notice that all variables exhibit a non-linear182
relation to our regressand, which is monotonic for some variables and non-monotonic for others. Figure 5183
showcases how some factors influence variation in the perception of safety:184

they have a negative effect on safety perceptions, suggesting that the image showcases a road-heavy192
environment, usually viewed as unsafer for cyclists as they do not have dedicated infrastructure to193

5. Discussion279
The present study examined the non-linear effects of various urban characteristics on the perception of280
cycling safety, shedding light on the complex interplay between urban environment factors and individuals’281
subjective safety assessments. Our findings have important implications for urban planning and the promo-282
tion of cycling as a sustainable mode of transportation, in addition to how street-view images can be used283
to study this topic.284
The results of this study revealed that the relationship between urban characteristics and cycling safety285
perception is not strictly linear. While some variables, such as the presence of dedicated or shared bike lanes286
and the existence of various types of street furniture, displayed a clear and consistent influence on safety287
perception, others showed a more subtle pattern. For example, the presence of other cyclists, road lane mark-288
ings, and snow had apparent non-linear effects, suggesting that there may be an optimal threshold beyond289
which additional improvements do not significantly enhance safety perception. These findings emphasize290
the importance of considering the interaction between multiple factors in urban design rather than focusing291
on individual characteristics. More, some particular results, such as those pertaining to urban form, suggest292
that further in-depth analysis needs to be conducted to analyze the impact of urban network-extracted data293
on the perception of cycling safety.294

While our study provides valuable insights, it is important to acknowledge some limitations. First, the311
perception of safety is subjective and can be influenced by individual characteristics, past experiences, and312
cultural factors, something not explored here. Future research should explore these aspects in greater detail313
to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the phenomenon. Additionally, longitudinal studies314
and more extensive data collection efforts could offer a more robust analysis of the non-linear relationships315
discussed here.316
Overall, the non-linear effects identified in this study underscore the need for tailored urban planning317
strategies that consider the unique characteristics and needs of looking at urban spaces as a whole. Urban318
environments are diverse, and the factors affecting cycling safety perception may vary greatly from one319
location to another. Engaging with local communities to understand their specific concerns and preferences320
and design interventions accordingly is also important.321
6. Conclusions322
In this work, we have explored how different urban elements impact cycling safety perceptions. For our323
analysis, we model both mapping and image-extracted data using an explainable boosting machine. This324
methodology allows us to understand the non-linear effects of various urban characteristics on individuals’325
perceptions, uncover pairwise interactions between different factors, and, at the same time, model a vast326
number of factors jointly, something often overlooked in the literature. Our analysis uncovered several327
essential insights, suggesting that such non-linear analysis is crucial in understanding individuals’ perceptions328
when evaluating the sense of safety from street-view images. Recognizing the complex interplay of urban329
factors and tailoring interventions is essential for creating cities conducive to safe and enjoyable cycling330
experiences.331
In the future, we plan to use the approach detailed here to analyze other cities and understand if the332
perception of safety can be generalized across cities with different urban characteristics or cycling cultures.333
Expanding the research to encompass a broader geographic scope can help identify regional variations in334
the non-linear effects of urban factors on cycling safety perception. Finally, understanding the impact of335
individuals’ cycling profiles and how they perceive cycling safety differently can also be valuable in addressing336
individuals’ needs better.337
