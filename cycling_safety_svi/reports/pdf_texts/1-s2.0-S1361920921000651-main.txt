ABSTRACT  
E-scooter is an innovative travel mode that meets the demand of many travelers. A lack of un -
derstanding of user routing preferences makes it difficult for policymakers to adapt existing in -
frastructures to accommodate these emerging travel demands. This study develops an e-scooter 
route choice model to reveal riders ’ preferences for different types of transportation in -
frastructures, using revealed preferences data. The data were collected using Global Positioning 
System units installed on e-scooters operating on Virginia Tech ’ s campus. We applied the 
Recursive Logit route choice model to 2000 randomly sampled e-scooter trajectories. The model 
results suggest e-scooter riders are willing to travel longer distances to ride in bikeways (59% 
longer), multi-use paths (29%), tertiary roads (15%), and one-way roads (21%). E-scooter users 
also prefer shorter and simpler routes. Finally, slope is not a determinant for e-scooter route 
choice, likely because e-scooters are powered by electricity.   
1. Introduction 
E-scooter is an innovative transportation mode that meets the travel demand of many travelers by providing flexible, affordable, 
and accessible mobility services. E-scooters are well-suited to replace trips shorter than 2 miles, which are 36% of all trips in the U.S. 
( U.S. Department of Transportation & Federal Highway Administration, 2019 ). Currently, the majority of these short trips, i.e., 
approximately 66% (i.e., calculated using weighted 2017 National Households Travel Survey [NHTS] data [ U.S. Department of 
Transportation & Federal Highway Administration, 2019 ]), are made by private automobiles. E-scooters have proven popular with the 
public, with usage increasing at a much faster rate than other forms of shared mobility (i.e., cars, bicycles) ( Clewlow, 2019 ). In 2018, 
there were 85,000 e-scooters available for public use in 100 U.S. cities, resulting in 38.5 million trips ( NACTO, 2018 ). 
In response to growing demand and e-scooter usage, many states and cities have passed laws and regulations to govern the 
deployment of shared fleets and use of e-scooters. Since the launch of the first shared e-scooter fleet program in 2017, the majority of U. 
S. states have enacted laws to allow the operation of e-scooters on streets, with some restrictions, such as speed limits, no e-scooters on 
sidewalks, operation time, driver ’ s license requirements, age constraints, etc. (Unagi Scooters, 2019 ). Some states, such as Kentucky

Local policymakers, however, face the challenge of providing safe and convenient environments to accommodate this emerging 
travel mode. City transportation planners seeking to adapt the current transportation system for e-scooters must develop an under -
standing of usage patterns and user needs. Importantly, there is a limited understanding regarding infrastructure preferences of e- 
scooter riders due to data limitations, especially the lack of revealed preference data. To the authors’ best knowledge, no study, to date, 
has been devoted to systematically modeling e-scooter user infrastructure preferences using empirical data. 
E-scooter riders may have different infrastructure preferences compared with bicyclists. Bicycles are more stable and perform 
better on rougher roads, due to their geometry and tire size. With smaller tires and their unique geometry designs, e-scooters require a 
more upright steering angle, which makes them less stable on roads with rough surfaces, such as pebbles and bumps. Meanwhile, e- 
scooter riders may find roads with steep slopes less intimidating, as riding an e-scooter does not require physical effort. Additionally, e- 
scooters are also smaller and lighter than bikes, making them easier to switch between sidewalk and street. However, no prior studies 
have explored the e-scooter riders’ infrastructure preferences to provide insights for infrastructure planning for e-scooters. 
This study develops an e-scooter route choice model using revealed preference data collected using Global Position System (GPS) 
units installed on e-scooters on Virginia Tech Blacksburg Campus. The model results show relative preferences for different trans -
portation infrastructures, as well as the effect of route complexity and topography on route choice. The remainder of the paper is 
organized as follows. In Section 2, we review existing literature on e-scooter and bicycle route choice preferences to highlight research 
gaps. In Section 3, we describe the modeling techniques and data used in this study. We provide model results and interpret e-scooter 
riders’ preferences in transportation infrastructure in Section 4. Finally, we conclude the major contribution of this paper and discuss 
future research directions in Section 5. 
2. Prior studies on e-scooter and bicycle route preferences 
Research about e-scooters is still nascent, given this innovative travel mode has just emerged recently. Most of the existing e-scooter 
studies are purely descriptive (BCDOT, 2019; Chowdhury et al., 2019; Jiao & Bai, 2020; Liu et al., 2019; Noland, 2019; PBOT, 2018). E- 
scooter demand and mode choice studies are biased by small sample size (Degele et al., 2018; Smith & Schwieterman, 2018) or hy -
pothetical assumptions in travel behavior (Lee et al., 2019). Zou et al. (2020) descriptively identified street infrastructures with high e- 
scooter demands and corridors that need proactive safety management using e-scooter trajectories and bike crash data collected from 
Washington D.C. 
There has been no e-scooter route choice modeling effort using revealed preference data to understand riders’ preferences for 
transportation infrastructures. Therefore, in this study, we reviewed the existing route choice modeling efforts for cyclists to inform the 
development of an e-scooter route choice model. The two travel modes share some similarities and are treated equally in some states. 
Cyclist route choice studies have evolved in the last three decades due to the advancements in survey methods and modeling 
techniques and the availability of location data. Early studies conducted descriptive analyses using simple stated or revealed pref -
erence surveys to understand the type of transportation infrastructures to prioritize future investments and promote bike usage 
(Antonakos, 1994; Aultman-Hall et al., 1997; Axhausen & Smith Jr, 1986; Davis, 1995; Guttenplan & Patten, 1995; Lott et al., 1978; 
Sacks, 1994). Built upon these initial analysis, several studies have developed discrete choice models, such as Binary and Multinomial 
logit models, to examine the tradeoffs between travel time and characteristics of transportation networks, such as the availability of a 
bicycle lane, types of bicycle lanes (e.g., protected vs. unprotected), as well as supportive bicycle infrastructures at the destinations, 
such as secured storage and showers (Abraham et al., 2002; Aultman-Hall et al., 1997; de Dios Ortúzar & Willumsen, 2011; Hunt & 
Abraham, 2007; Landis et al., 1994; Sener et al., 2009; Stinson & Bhat, 2003; Taylor & Mahmassani, 1996). These discrete choice 
models were developed based on stated choice experiment surveys, which may have hypothetical and self-selection biases that need to 
be controlled using additional attitudinal variables. 
The increasing uptake of smartphones and availability of Geographic Information Systems (GIS), and Global Positioning System 
(GPS) technologies later enabled discrete choice modeling using revealed preference data. For instance, Menghini et al. (Menghini 
et al., 2010), Hood et al. (Hood et al., 2011), and Broach et al. (Broach et al., 2012) developed bike route choice models using GPS data 
from Zurich (Switzerland), California (US), and Portland (Oregon, US). These discrete choice models were developed using a path- 
based modeling method. For each pair of origins and destinations, a restricted set of route choice alternatives was first developed 
using choice set generation algorithms, such as K-shortest paths, simulated shortest paths, and route labeling. Utility functions were 
then estimated given the generated choice set. The disadvantage of such a modeling method is that the estimation results may vary 
significantly depending on the adopted route choice set generation algorithm (Broach et al., 2012). However, it is impossible to explore 
all potential choice sets in the model, given the exponential number of paths in the network. More recently, to overcome these lim -
itations, a new genre of discrete choice models was proposed, including Recursive Logistic (RL) (Fosgerau et al., 2013) and Nested RL 
models (Mai et al., 2015). Zimmermann et al. (2017) applied the latest RL models to understand bicycle route preferences and pre -
dicted link-level bicycle volume. More advanced computational algorithms, such as the decomposition method, were also proposed to 
effectively solve the RL and Nested RL models (Mai et al., 2018). 
Although the modeling methodology and used data sources have advanced substantially, the findings from prior studies conducted 
in different cities over the world remain quite consistent. In general, it is found that cyclists prefer routes with more bikeway in -
frastructures, such as protected, well maintained, and connected bike lanes and intersections with bicycle signals and/or painted lanes. 
Some studies also found that routes that are more aesthetically pleasant are more preferred by cyclists. Meanwhile, cyclists also tend to

Motivated by the research gap, we developed a route choice model using e-scooter GPS trajectory data from Virginia Tech

choice model with selected network link-level characteristics, namely the presence of different infrastructures (e.g., cycleways, multi-

left turns and U-turns). The model outcomes can shine light on infrastructure planning on U.S. campuses to proactively prepare the 
system to the introduction of e-scooters.

3.1. Recursive logit model 
A Recursive Logit (RL) route choice model is a choice model with an unrestricted choice set that can be used to determine travelers ’ 
transportation infrastructure preferences. Prior studies on revealed route preferences for drivers and cyclists primarily use conven -
tional discrete choice models with the assumption that the restricted choice set contains the actual travel paths considered by travelers. 
These studies have devoted substantial attention to the generation of robust choice sets, as the results may vary significantly based on 
the selected algorithm ( Broach et al., 2012 ). Realistic choice set generation for bicyclists are found to be more challenging compared 
with automobiles, due to higher network density and the large number of variables influencing cyclists ’ route selection. Such difficulty 
also applies to the e-scooter route choice set development. More advanced choice set generation algorithms have been developed and 
implemented for cyclists recently ( Broach et al., 2012; Hood et al., 2011; Menghini et al., 2010 ). However, these advanced algorithms 
still cannot exhaust all considered paths in the constrained choice set ( Broach et al., 2012 ). The recently introduced RL model 
overcomes such limitations. The RL model can consistently estimate route preferences with a dynamic choice set ( Mai et al., 2015; 
Zimmermann et al., 2017 ). The RL model is formulated as Equation (1) ( Fosgerau et al., 2013 ).

3.2. E-scooter trajectory data 
This study uses GPS observations of 76,652 e-scooter trips on Virginia Tech Blacksburg Campus collected from September to 
October 2019. It was conducted as part of an 18-month research partnership between Virginia Tech Transportation Institute and Ford 
subsidiary Spin, an e-scooter mobility provider. The study included an in-app survey at the end of each ride inviting riders to contribute 
their data for research purposes. Therefore, this dataset represents a sample of trips from riders who gave consent to participate. 
A fleet of 200 Spin e-scooters launched on Virginia Tech campus in 2019. The operation of the e-scooters is restricted to the Virginia 
Tech Blacksburg campus (an area of approximately one square mile) using geofencing technology (i.e., if a rider takes an e-scooter out 
of the campus operation boundary, the e-scooter will turn off and provide audible guidance to return to the approved riding area). E- 
scooters cannot operate in the Town of Blacksburg due to the absence of operating permissions from the Town. Additionally, riders are 
not allowed to bring e-scooters inside campus buildings or parking garages, except for ground parking lots. The e-scooters were 
deployed every day from 7:00 am until 30 min after dusk, but removed in advance of high-traffic events, during winter break, and

The campus community consists of 34,000 students and 7,500 faculty and staff. A survey of e-scooter users indicated that they are 
predominately students (90%), younger than 24 years (86%), white (69%), male (59%) and live off campus (59%) ( Virginia Tech 
Transportation Institute, 2020 ). The top types of trips reported by users were going to/from class or a class-related activity (29%), 
parking areas (11%), the library, gym, or health center (11%), social/entertainment activities, (8%), dining (6%), shopping (2%), and 
riding for fun/recreation (20%). A large share of users (45%) reported using e-scooters once a week or more frequently. The top 
reasons reported by non-users for not wanting to use an e-scooter were that they were happy with their current form of transportation 
(39%), e-scooters were not convenient (17%), and they didn ’ t seem safe (16%). 
The survey included stated preference questions asking e-scooter users both what type of transportation infrastructure they 
preferred to ride on and what infrastructure they actually used, regardless of their preferences. As shown in Fig. 1 , most e-scooter riders 
preferred to ride on sidewalks and in bike lanes in the street, and least preferred shared travel lanes with cars and campus trails and 
footpaths. However, they reported actually using sidewalks and shared travel lanes more than preferred, which may be due to the fact 
that they are not comfortable interacting with pedestrians and cars. Surveys collected from other road users also suggest that pe -
destrians perceive it as unsafe to share sidewalks with e-scooters ( James et al., 2019 ). The VT survey results also suggest e-scooter users 
ride on on-street bike lanes and off-street trails less than preferred, perhaps due to a lack of availability of the preferred infrastructure 
types. There are only approximately nine miles of bike lanes located on the west side of the campus. 
E-scooter trajectory data were collected using the default GPS units on the e-scooters, which record locations in the form of latitude 
and longitude every 5 s. The saved GPS coordinates have five decimal digits of precision. To protect users ’ privacy, the GPS trajectory 
points located in the first and last 50 feet of the trajectory were removed prior to analysis. Short trips (i.e., trips shorter than 100 m) and 
trips that are recreational or leisure purposes (i.e., round trips with destinations located within 100 m from the origins) were also 
removed from the dataset. We removed these trips because their route choices are typically very different from regular trips (i.e., 
getting from location A to B). This is a particular problem for e-scooters route preference modeling rather than for bicycles because 
many riders are new to the technology and are in the try-it-out stage and some use the mode for leisure trips as the primary purpose. 
Some other studies also excluded this type of trip in the analysis (e.g., Zou et al., 2020 ). 
GPS trajectory point data were then post-processed to generate routes before feeding data into the RL model. The observed GPS 
locations were map-matched to OpenStreetMap (OSM) links using the Open Source Routing Machine (OSRM) ’ s map-matching al -
gorithm and Python 3.0. In this process, the collected GPS points were snapped to the most “ likely ” OSM links to generate the route 
taken by e-scooter riders. OSRM map-matching algorithm estimates link matching likelihood based on (1) the distance from the GPS 
point to the link, and (2) the difference in travel speed. The link-level travel speed is mode specific and predefined in three existing 
travel profiles, namely car, bicycle, and walk. The bike profile is used because e-scooter and bicycles share more average travel speed 
on different infrastructures, compared with automobiles and pedestrians. 
The OSRM map-matching results also include a confidence measurement, which indicates the reliability of the map-matching 
result. Examples of map-matching results with different confidence values are shown in Table 2 . We manually examined the map- 
matching results across different confidence level. We found that the map-matching results are not accurate, when the confidence 
level is very small (e.g., 0.05). To control the quality of the map-matching results, routes with confidence level less than 0.5 are 
removed from further analysis. The Fig. 2 shows the density of filtered map-matched e-scooter routes on campus. 
Finally, we randomly selected 2000 (i.e., approximately 10.2% of the remaining trips) of the remaining e-scooter trips across 
campus for route choice modeling. The random sampling process is used to save computational time, as it is more expensive to solve RL 
models with large number of sampled trips because they are estimated based on dynamic choice sets. The sample should be sufficient 
for the modeling purpose, as it is larger than or similar to the sample size used in most city-level bicycle route choice models. Two

3.3. Transportation infrastructure attributes

carefully constructed. For example, it is not feasible to directly include slope as a link-level continuous variable into the model, because

The following link-level attributes are used for model estimation, including length, slope, presence of different types of trans -
portation infrastructures, turning angles along the roads and at intersections, etc. Specifically, Virginia Tech transportation in -
frastructures are classified into ten categories in the OSM network, namely bikeways, multi-use paths, pedestrian-exclusive ways, 
primary roads, tertiary roads, service roads, steps, one-way roads, bridges, and tunnels. The facility classification in OSM is slightly 
different from the user survey conducted on campus. The OSM does not have indicators suggesting if a road has separated sidewalk or 
not. However, most tertiary roads, one-way roads, and local roads on campus are equipped with sidewalks to accommodate pedes -
trians. Pedestrian ways and steps in the OSM dataset, on the other hand, are actually off-street campus trails and footpaths that locates 
adjacent to academic buildings and residential halls. Meanwhile, the definitions of bikeways and on-street bike lanes are consistent. 
Slope and transportation infrastructure variables are transformed to be link additive intersected terms. For the slope variable, we 
examined dummies for different upslope thresholds, including above 4%, 5%, and 6%, based on existing cycling route preferences 
literature. Left turn and U-turn variables are intersected with the intersection dummy to exclude significant changes in direction along 
the same road. We also created an intersected term of change in direction and none intersection dummy to measure how winding the 
routes are. Table 3 summarizes the tested link-level attributes x ( a | k ) of each link pair ( k , a ) that are examined in this study. 
Fig. 3 displays the spatial distribution of different types of transportation infrastructure on VT ’ s campus. Bikeways (i.e., dark green 
bolded lines) are primarily distributed on the west side of the campus surrounding the golf course, duck pond, the Virginia Tech Inn, 
and the Greek fraternity and sorority houses. There are approximately nine miles of bikeways on campus. Pedestrian-exclusive 
walkways are primarily located adjacent to residential and academic buildings. Multi-use paths (i.e., light green dashed lines) 
dominate the east side of the campus, where the residential halls and academic buildings are located. The multi-use paths, however, 
are frequently disconnected by stairs (i.e., see dark red lines). The majority of the roads on campus are tertiary or secondary roads (i.e., 
black lines) and service roads (i.e., blue lines). The service roads are primarily located at the back entrances of the buildings or on 
surface parking lots. There is a very limited number of primary roads at the boundary of the campus. There are several one-way roads 
on campus (i.e., bolded orange lines), including the “ Drill Field road ” circling the largest green space at the center of the campus. There 
are also 43 bridges (connecting academic buildings, residential halls, and parking lots) and 12 tunnels (typically as a part of an outside 
walkway through large buildings) on campus. There are 1982 links (i.e., ~18% of links in the road network) on campus with upward 
slope larger than 4%.

4. Results and discussion 
The model is estimated using Matlab by adjusting an existing open-sourced code developed by Tien Mai ( https://github.com/ 
maitien86/RecursiveLogit.Classical.V2 ). There are 10,856 OSM network links and 7362 nodes on Virginia Tech ’ s campus. It takes 
approximately 3 h to finish estimating one configuration of the RL model on a computer with 8 Intel(R) Core i7 @ 3.1 GHz cores and 
16G of memory. 
Table 4 shows the model estimation results for variables that are significant at 90% significant level. In Model 1, all available 
variables, except for the Link Size (LS) variable, are included in the model. Some variables, such as the interaction term of link length 
and upward slope dummy (no matter what threshold we selected), pedestrian way, primary, service road, tunnel, and bridge, are found 
to be not statistically significant. We iteratively remove the most insignificant variables from the model and then re-estimate it until all 
variables in the model are significant. In Model 2, all variables are significant and have the expected signs, according to the existing 
bicycle route choice models. Model 3 shows the results after including the LS attribute into the model. The LS attribute is also highly 
significant. The estimated coefficients are quite similar between Model 2 and 3 in terms of magnitude and sign. The following section 
interprets model results from Model 3. 
In general, the results here agree with the literature review above and the results of the stated preference survey conducted on VT 
campus, suggesting e-scooter users and bicyclists tend to share preferences for transportation infrastructures. For instance, e-scooter 
riders tend to prefer shorter routes, as the estimated coefficients for link length and link constant variables are negative and highly 
significant. Most of the existing bicycle route choice models also found these variables to be negatively correlated with the route utility, 
as shown in Table 1 in the literature review section. Unlike cyclists, e-scooter users are not sensitive to the slope of the road, as the 
coefficients for the upslope interaction term is not significant. This is expected because e-scooters are powered by electric motors. 
E-scooter riders tend to favor bikeways and multi-use paths. In Model 3, the estimated coefficient for the Length parameter in -
creases from   115.92 to   46.85 on road segments with bikeways, (i.e., after adding the coefficient for the Bikeway parameter, +
69.07) in Model 3. Thus, e-scooter riders perceive routes with bikeways to be 59% shorter than those without such an amenity. 
Similarly, the value of the length parameter increases from   115.92 to   83.67 on road segments dedicated to multi-use paths. This 
suggests a road segment with a multi-use path is perceived to be 28% shorter than a segment without this amenity. 
The local road segments on campus are also well-perceived by e-scooter users. The estimated coefficients for tertiary and one-way 
roads are 17.26 and 24.03 correspondingly, indicating riders are willing to travel longer to use tertiary roads and one-way roads. The 
perceived road length is reduced by 15% and 21% for tertiary and one-way roads, correspondingly. However, the magnitudes of 
perceived distance reduction are smaller compared to those for bicycle infrastructures. The maximum vehicle speed allowed on 
campus roads is 15 miles per hour, and car and truck drivers on campus are well-aware of cyclists and pedestrians, making e-scooter 
users feel safe and convenient to travel on these roads. This is consistent with the existing riding observations from Portland, OR, that e- 
scooter users ’ willingness to use a road is negatively associated with posted speed limit ( PBOT, 2018 ). Additionally, e-scooters users 
may actually ride on the sidewalks of the roads, which is more preferred than riding on on-street travel lanes according to the VT e- 
scooter campus survey results, shown in Fig. 1 . The preference for riding e-scooters on local roads may change in off-campus 
neighborhoods with low cyclist and pedestrian volumes or without sidewalks. Future studies may need to explore the impact of the 
presence of sidewalks and bikelanes, traffic volume, and vehicle travel speeds on e-scooter users ’ route choices.

scooter or ride dangerously. As a result, the estimated coefficient for stairs is negative and significant. The value of the coefficient on

E-scooter users are insensitive to the following infrastructures: pedestrian-exclusive ways, primary roads, service roads, tunnels, 
and bridges. The on-campus survey results, however, show most e-scooter riders tend to avoid using pedestrian-exclusive ways, i.e., 
footpaths. The RL model results suggest this may be associated with the fact that these footpaths are interrupted by steps, which has a 
very significant negative sign. The insignificant estimation of primary roads may be attributed to the fact that there is a very limited 
number of primary roads on Virginia Tech’s campus. Additionally, e-scooter users do not need to follow service roads to navigate 
through parking lots. As a result, these routes are typically not successfully map-matched to OSM network, which means they may have 
been excluded from the sample analyzed. The bridges and tunnels on campus are used to access buildings rather than for through- 
travel purposes. The e-scooter geofencing technology prevents the use of e-scooters in academic buildings and parking garages on 
campus, rendering it less likely for users to ride e-scooters on the majority of bridges or through tunnels. 
Finally, the model also suggests that e-scooter users tend to select simpler routes, i.e., routes with fewer left turns, and tend to avoid 
U-turns and winding routes, which are consistent with existing bicycle route choice model results. This may be because winding routes 
with frequent U-turns are typically longer than simpler routes. 
These revealed preference results agree with the stated preferences reported by e-scooter users in the survey and give further 
insights. Riders reported using sidewalks and shared travel lanes with cars more than preferred, which may be largely to do with taking 
the shortest route. However, the model results imply that e-scooter riders will make detours to use routes with preferred infrastructure 
(bikeways, multi-use paths, and tertiary or one-way roads) or avoid other infrastructure (stairs). 
5. Conclusion 
In this study, we developed recursive logit (RL) route choice models to examine infrastructure preferences of e-scooter users using 
GPS trajectory data collected on Virginia Tech campus from September to October 2019. A sample of 2000 e-scooter trips was 
analyzed. The final model provides rich insights on e-scooter users’ preferences on different transportation infrastructures, which have 
not been examined before due to a lack of revealed preference data. 
The results suggest e-scooter users are willing to travel 59% and 28% longer on segments with bikeways and multi-use paths. Local 
roads, such as tertiary roads or one-way roads (including roads with wide medians to separate right of the ways) are also found to be 
attractive, as the perceived travel distances are reduced by 15% and 21% correspondingly. Bikeways display the largest positive 
estimated coefficients and are the most preferred infrastructures. Stairs on walkways are considered as unattractive, and e-scooter 
users are willing to travel 55% longer to avoid them. The coefficients of other infrastructures, such as pedestrian ways, primary and 
service roads, bridges, and tunnels, are found to be statistically insignificant, indicating e-scooter users are not sensitive to these 
amenities on Virginia Tech campus. This may be attributed to the fact that there is a limited amount of these transportation in -
frastructures on campus and e-scooter users are not allowed to travel in the Town of Blacksburg. Bridges and tunnels on campus are 
typically designed to connect indoor spaces (e.g., classrooms, parking decks, etc.) rather than for traveling or commuting purposes. 
Therefore, e-scooter use may be restricted on the majority of these infrastructures through the geofencing technology. Similar to 
cyclists, e-scooter users also tend to take shorter and simpler routes, and to avoid left turns and U-turns. However, different from the 
findings of bicycle route choice models, the upward slope of the segment is not negatively perceived, likely because e-scooters are 
powered by an electric motor. 
The e-scooter route choice and stated preference survey results have the following policy implications for infrastructure planning. 
E-scooter riders prefer infrastructures that are separated from cars, such as bike lanes and mixed-use trails. Therefore, more bikeways 
should be introduced on campus to accommodate the demand. Even though e-scooter riders like to ride on sidewalks, they tend to ride 
on sidewalks more often than they prefer according to the stated preference survey, possibly due to the lack of bicycle infrastructure. E- 
scooter riders also tend to avoid footpaths and stairs, as they may experience difficulty balancing the vehicle and have safety concerns. 
Therefore, e-scooter infrastructure planning should encourage smoother surfaces and minimizing obstacles on the path to reduce safety 
concerns. 
The contributions of this work can be extended to gain a deeper understanding of route preferences for e-scooter users. First, the 
current model does not include any demographic and socio-economic variables to determine if preferences vary across different users, 
these data were not available. However, future modeling efforts should examine the heterogeneity in e-scooter user preferences. 
Second, this study does not exhaust all infrastructure related variables due to limited types of transportation infrastructures on the 
Virginia Tech campus. For instance, this study does not further distinguish bikeway types (e.g., protected vs. unprotected) given the 
limited number of bikeways on Virginia Tech’s campus and does not model sidewalks separately. Additionally, existing bicycle route 
choice studies have found that the preference for infrastructure can also be influenced by automobile and truck traffic volume and 
speed, as well as intersection designs. Therefore, future modeling efforts should incorporate more refined infrastructure variables to 
determine the sensitivity of e-scooter users. Moreover, the accuracy of GPS points used in this study is limited to five digits after the 
decimals, which are not sufficient to identify the lane that e-scooter riders have used. Studies developed using GPS points with higher 
resolutions should be developed to understand user preferences on roads with different traffic lanes. Finally, the current model is 
developed using GPS data generated primarily by young college students. The infrastructure preferences may be different for e-scooter 
users outside of the campus setting. The infrastructure characteristics may also vary significantly in urban settings. Therefore, more 
empirical studies are needed in larger cities with different urban forms, built environments, and transportation infrastructures to 
obtain more generalizable e-scooter route choice preferences.
