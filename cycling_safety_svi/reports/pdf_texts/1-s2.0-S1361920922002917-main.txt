ABSTRACT  
Knowing about the specific route preferences of demographic groups is crucial when researching 
equity in cycling. In this study, we use a large-scale stated-preference experiment with 4389 
respondents and 35,112 choice observations to evaluate route choice behavior. We set up discrete 
route choice models and focus on the specific needs of women, people cycling with children, and 
older adults. Results show that all groups of interest show higher, but not fundamentally different, 
route requirements. In this context, lower speed limits for motorized transport are very important 
for people cycling with children, and older adults. In addition, women and those traveling with 
children attach significantly higher value to separated bike infrastructure. These results show that 
vulnerable road users benefit stronger from a bike-friendly road infrastructure than the cyclist 
population as a whole.   
1. Introduction 
Unlike motorized transport, cycling has major advantages regarding emissions of CO
2
, pollutants, and further negative externalities 
( Makarova, Mavrin, Magdin, Shubenkova, & Boyko, 2019 ). Cycling as a cost-efficient, healthy mode of transport is also beneficial for 
the individual ( Lee et al., 2012; Mueller et al., 2015 ). Physical inactivity is known to be a major cause of chronic diseases and 
significantly harms public health ( Blair, 2009; Ding et al., 2016 ). At the same time, research has demonstrated strong positive in -
terrelations between a better quality and quantity of the bike infrastructure and cycling levels ( Buehler & Pucher, 2011; Hardinghaus, 
Nieland, Lehne, & Weschke, 2021; Winters, Teschke, Brauer, & Fuller, 2016 ). Consequently, better bike infrastructure is associated 
with improvements in public health ( Mueller et al., 2018 ). Thus, many cities all over the world are improving cycling conditions and 
investing in bike infrastructure ( Lanzendorf & Busch-Geertsema, 2014 ). 
In recent scientific literature, however, we are experiencing growing concerns regarding the inequity of the developments 
described. The main question in this discussion is whether all groups in society have the same access to good bike infrastructure and 
may benefit from the redesign of the streetscape ( Sheller, 2015 ). These equity concerns slowly started to attract more attention when 
the redesign of the urban road infrastructure picked up speed at the beginning of the 21st century. Before that, in many places the 
concept of vehicular cycling opposed separated bike infrastructure ( Forester, 1993 ). Accordingly, the group accommodated by such a 
concept consisted mainly of middle-aged males ( Schultheiss, Sanders, & Toole, 2018 ). 
The research body associated with equity of the bike infrastructure is currently growing. Mora et al. (2021) evaluate the acces -
sibility of bike infrastructure in Santiago de Chile. The authors focus on income and conclude that wealthier groups have better access

(Tucker & Manaugh, 2018). Similar results are also obtained from multi-city approaches in the US (Braun, Rodriguez, & Gordon-

infrastructure increases the share of female cyclists (AitBihiOuali & Klingen, 2022). 
On the other hand, different route preferences for various sociodemographic indicators are also of interest. There is a large body of 
research on cyclists’ route preferences using various research approaches. Previous studies use stated-preference choice experiments 
(Clark, Mokhtarian, Circella, & Watkins, 2019; Winters & Teschke, 2010), revealed preference approaches (Broach, Dill, & Gliebe, 
2012; Hardinghaus & Nieland, 2021; Pritchard, 2018) or qualitative approaches (Barratt, 2017; Van Cauwenberg et al., 2018). Thus, 
not all studies focus on investigating different preferences for relevant subgroups or even differentiate between them. When consid -
ered, results differ accordingly. In a review of 54 stated-preference studies, Aldred et al. (2016) conclude that women clearly have a 
stronger preference for separated infrastructure than men do (Aldred, Elliott, Woodcock, & Goodman, 2016). Vedel et al. (2017) find 
similar results (Vedel, Jacobsen, & Skov-Petersen, 2017). Results also indicate a stronger preference for separated infrastructure 
among older adults but, due to contradictory findings of the studies included, evidence is limited (Aldred et al., 2016). Other stated- 
preference studies find little difference between the route choice of women and men (Clark et al., 2019; Rowangould & Tayarani, 
2016). Winters and Teschke (2010) investigate route preferences of “the near market for cycling” and find higher route requirements 
among women as well as people who cycle less often (Winters & Teschke, 2010). 
Other research defines the subgroup of interest beforehand and focuses on the specific needs of this group (Garrard, Handy, & Dill, 
2012; Ghekiere et al., 2014). In a qualitative approach, Russel et al. (2021) reveal a high burden of women’s safety concerns regarding 
cycling in New Zealand (Russell, Davies, Wild, & Shaw, 2021). Desjardins et al. (2021) in Canada (Desjardins et al., 2021) present 
another qualitative approach for the case of a city catching up in bike infrastructure. In Belgium, Van Cauwenberg et al. (2018) use 
bike-along interviews to figure out environmental themes that influence seniors’ cycling experience (Van Cauwenberg et al., 2018). 
Bike-along interviews are also used by Ghekiere et al. (2014) to investigate environmental factors that influence cycling among 
children (Ghekiere et al., 2014). Such approaches deliver valuable and detailed insights into the specific groups but have limitations 
when it comes to comparability with the majority of society. Earlier studies also have limitations regarding the sample size, the specific 
local spatial context, and the integration of comprehensive as well as innovative street properties. As a consequence, it remains unclear 
to what extent the route preferences of certain groups differ from the majority. This is crucial when aiming to infer recommendations 
for planning and practice with regard to municipal cycle strategies. In addition, this knowledge enables us to evaluate bike-friendly city 
development, and in particular its equity conditions, from the perspective of subgroups. 
On one hand, equity in the supply of bicycle infrastructure means a spatially equal distribution that all neighborhoods benefit from. 
On the other hand, it means an infrastructure that is designed in such a way that different groups benefit equally given their specific 
needs. We can only fully evaluate the equity in supply of bicycle infrastructure if we have better knowledge of these specific needs in all 
groups of society. This is particularly important, since transport planners are often predominantly middle-aged males which may lead 
to disadvantages for women in the production of space (Burgess, 2008; Little, 1994; Tummers & Wankiewicz, 2020). Hence, it is 
crucial to evaluate whether the claims defined and the decisions made meet the demand of groups with less inclusion. At the same time, 
earlier research was aimed at large-scale quantitative studies to evaluate influences on cycling experience in subgroups (Van Cau -
wenberg et al., 2018). 
Hence, in this study we conduct a large-scale choice experiment right across Germany to investigate route preferences for various 
relevant road properties. In the evaluation, we focus on the requirements of women, seniors and those cycling with children, since 
literature suggests that these groups have specific needs regarding infrastructure and corresponding route preferences.

2.1. Sample 
The sample used for the analysis in this paper includes all the observations of respondents from Germany who provided information 
regarding their age, gender and whether or not they cycle with children. This leads to a total of 4,389 respondents which adds up to 
35,112 choice observations from the experiment. As this paper estimates preferences for infrastructure characteristics when riding a 
bike for different sociodemographic groups, Table 2 shows distributions of age, gender, frequency of riding a bike, and cycling with 
children for the full sample but also separated according to the user groups analyzed. When compared with national travel survey data, 
it can be confirmed that there are more male cyclists than female cyclists in Germany, even if the nine percentage points difference is 
much smaller than in this sample ( Nobis, 2019 ). Compared to the German population, the overall frequency of cycling is much higher 
in the sample. In Germany, 18% of the population cycle daily, while 36% never or almost never cycle ( Nobis, 2019 ). In the present 
sample, these groups consist of 75% and 0.8%, respectively. Considering the German population as a whole, cycling levels are highest 
among teenagers, while in the age groups between 20 and 79 years, the share of regular cyclists (cycling at least once a month) differs 
by only four percentage points ( Nobis, 2019 ). Hence, young adults are overrepresented in the present sample. 
When looking at the sample distributions of the respective user groups, distribution of age, frequency, and cycling with children are 
pretty similar in males and females. Some differences exist in the 18 to 24-year age group where the share of males is higher than the 
share of females which is reversed for the 45 to 54 and 55 to 64 year age groups. Also, at 16.5%, the share of women cycling with

factor of whether cycling with children or not particularly with regard to age, which is pretty much to be expected as it is mostly

25 to 54-year-olds. Distributions of other categories, however, match much more closely. Roughly 2.5% of the respondents are 65 years

To model differences between the user groups, four models were estimated. Model 1 is the base model, estimating parameters for 
the infrastructure characteristics as average for all respondents, disregarding their user group (i.e. no interaction terms or other 
recognition of sociodemographic variables). Models 2, 3 and 4 then estimate the parameters for infrastructure characteristics 
differentiated by the characteristics gender, cycling with children, and age, respectively, using interaction between the variables. First 
of all, in each model separate parameters were estimated for all variables and tested for significant difference at 5% level. Subse -
quently, only those parameters that differed significantly were remained separately in the model, while the others were estimated as 
joint parameters.

prefer bike paths more than males, but this does not hold true for protected bike lanes, and second, females prefer living streets more

In the following, some preference comparisons between the groups studied will be pointed out. They always represent the relation 
between the sociodemographic group under consideration and the outside group (e.g. cycling with children compared to cycling 
without children). Bike paths are preferred more by females compared to males, and by respondents cycling with children compared to 
those without. Protected bike lanes (bike lanes separated from motorized traffic by bollards) are favored 33% more among those 
cycling with children. Preferences for living streets (comparable to shared spaces, cars are only allowed to drive at walking speed) 
differ between gender and the factor of cycling with children. For the latter group, preferences for living streets are nearly tripled 
compared to the others while preferences for cycle streets are 30% higher. A speed limit of 30 km/h for cars is especially preferred by 
those cycling with children and older respondents, with 50% and 75% higher valuation, respectively. This is the same for side streets 
with around 25% and 50%, respectively, higher preferences compared to the other group. Preferences for asphalt compared to cob -
blestones are slightly higher for those without children than for those cycling with children. Preferences for on-street parking are the 
same for all groups analyzed. The presence of trees along the route, however, is favored by females compared to males, with no 
differences between the other two groups analyzed. 
For a better interpretation of the differences in preferences for infrastructure attributes of the respondent groups, the marginal rate 
of substitution between each attribute and travel time is calculated and presented in Table 4 . This means that the values in Table 4 
represent the amount of travel time (in minutes) that respondents are willing to invest (on average) in order to obtain the respective 
infrastructure attribute compared to the reference. For instance, the average population is willing to cycle up to nearly 9 min (8.93) 
longer to have a protected bike lane on this route. For females, this attribute is worth more than 10 min of extra travel time and those 
cycling with children are even willing to travel an extra 11.52 min, while those riding without children value the existence of a 
protected bike lane at only an extra 8.65 min of travel time. In the comparison of valuations in terms of travel time, it is interesting to 
note that the results of model 2 report a difference in parameter estimates for travel time between women and men, which is why the 
value of attributes in terms of travel time differs for each attribute even when there is no difference in the parameter for the attribute 
itself (e.g. bike lane). Due to the difference in parameter estimates, women are always willing to sacrifice more travel time in order to 
cycle on better infrastructure than men. Differences in valuation of travel time between men and women seem to be in line with 
previous literature findings on the value of travel time ( Schmid et al., 2019 ). A further advantage of standardizing the parameter values 
by travel time is the improvement in comparing the valuation between different attributes. While trees are valued nearly twice as high 
among females compared to males, the overall value is low, so the difference in travel time that they are willing to sacrifice for trees is 
only half a minute which is much lower than e.g. the value of bike lanes or bike paths. 
4. Discussion 
The results presented in the four models as shown above are meaningful regarding different preferences among user groups and 
show that different groups have different demands regarding bike infrastructure. The implications of the results and related recom -
mendations and policy implications will be highlighted in this section. 
Comparing the four models, several fundamental trends of different route preferences become obvious. First, all subgroups of 
interest (women, people cycling with children, and older adults) show higher route requirements than the whole sample. Significantly 
higher utilities are observed for different route characteristics. There is no case in which the subgroups show lower route requirements.

That means in the trade-off between route characteristics and travel time, the subgroups of interest value the route characteristics 
analyzed higher than the whole sample does. Second, people cycling with children value slower speeds of motorized transport much 
higher than the entire population. Riding in a living street is 1.7 times more highly favored by people cycling with children than by

limit of 30 km/h compared to 50 km/h. The same holds true for side streets which are preferred to a larger extent over main streets. 
Third, a similar tendency regarding the drawbacks of fast motorized transport is observed in respect of the group of older adults. They 
value a speed for cars of 30 km/h compared to 50 km/h which is 45% higher than the younger group. For even slower speeds, however, 
there are no significant differences. Fourth, separated bike-infrastructure (bike paths) is favored more by females than males and by 
people cycling with children (bike path and protected bike lanes). There are almost no significant differences regarding the road 
surface, trees along the road and parked cars, factors which can be understood as proxy for the comfort and attractiveness of the road. 
Only female cyclists value trees along the road higher than males do. A slightly lower preference for a smooth surface within the group 
of people cycling with children can be explained by generally having permission to cycle on the sidewalk when accompanying a bike- 
riding child under ten years old. 
Compared to earlier research, we find an even stronger preference for separated facilities among women. (Aldred et al., 2016; Vedel 
et al., 2017; Winters & Teschke, 2010). This finding is in line with current research, indicating that an increase in cycling infrastructure 
levels the gender balance of cyclists (AitBihiOuali & Klingen, 2022). The authors argue that safety is more important for women than 
for men. In addition, Grudgings et al. (2021) find that the presence of cycle lanes is more relevant for the share of female cyclists than 
for males (Grudgings, Hughes, & Hagen-Zanker, 2021). In the present research, however, this finding can only be supported for the 
case of bike paths. For protected bike lanes, we do not find any significant differences. In relation to other trade-offs, the gender 
variable is less strong. Hence, overall, we are in line with earlier research which concludes limited differences of route choice regarding 
gender (Clark et al., 2019; Rowangould & Tayarani, 2016). Regarding the limited evidence of stronger preferences for separated 
facilities among older people, the present study sees no significant differences (Aldred et al., 2016). Nevertheless, we provide evidence 
of a stronger preference for side streets and lower speed limits. Recent qualitative research supports calm residential streets and 
separated bicycle infrastructure as the preferred road environment for cyclists riding their bikes with children (Desjardins et al., 2021). 
Earlier research suggested that older people had a higher preference for separated infrastructure (Aldred et al., 2016). With regard to 
this, the present study observes higher route requirements for older cyclists, concluding a stronger preference for calm roads and lower 
speed limits, but it cannot prove a greater desire for separated infrastructures. 
The findings and their consideration in planning and practice are especially important, since older adults and children are 
vulnerable groups that deserve special protection. Providing a bike-friendly city environment with lower speed zones also makes the 
city more child-friendly. It allows for independent active mobility and therefore the social participation of minors. At the same time, 
the socialization of mobility behavior is very important for children, since it influences their behavior over their whole lifetime 
(Müggenburg, Busch-Geertsema, & Lanzendorf, 2015). Before being allowed to drive, children and teenagers show one of the highest 
cycling rates across all age groups (Nobis, 2019). A strategy attracting children to cycle even more and ensuring a comfortable and safe 
experience may therefore have long term effects on the mobility behavior of society. Regarding seniors, earlier research finds that they 
are more likely to be involved in accidents when cycling (Rodgers, 1997). Furthermore, serious injuries occur more often when ac -
cidents are sustained in older age (Dai & Dadashova, 2021; Yang, Yang, Smith, & Robert, 2021). These known higher risks for older 
adults correspond to their preference for lower speed limits. A good cycle strategy should enable seniors to minimize their individual 
risk by following their route preferences. Allowing older adults to cycle has a positive influence on their cognitive function and well- 
being (Leyland et al., 2019). 
Based on these findings, recommendations for planning and practice can be drawn. Most important finding of the present study is 
that all subgroups under consideration show higher route requirements than the mean population. That does not imply that the 
average population is not valuing proper bike infrastructure, but is less willing to give up travel time (i.e. taking a longer route) to ride 
on better infrastructure. Consequently, there are no fundamental differences in what an attractive bike-infrastructure looks like. The 
relevant bikeable road properties are just more important for the subgroups considered. This means that these groups benefit even 
more than the majority from a bike-friendly adaptation of urban road infrastructure. As a result, slower car speeds are desired to some 
extent by all subgroups considered. Hence, a strategy of lower speed limits, especially in the lower-level road network, would meet 
these needs and should therefore be integrated into strategic cycle plans. 
The limitations of the present study are discussed in the following. As the data collection approach relied mainly on social media 
and networks, we expected a self-selective sample of people interested in cycling and corresponding policies. The sample is also 
distorted in respect of sociodemographic variables as described in section 2. Although the sample size is very large for a choice 
experiment, the group of older adults accounts for a small share of the whole sample. We do not consider this problematic because the 
corresponding subsample is still large enough to identify significant differences. Finally, we can only evaluate road properties which 
were included in the experiment. There may be other attributes or other sociodemographic distinguishing features which would better 
explain the differences. 
5. Conclusion 
The specific needs of population groups who are less involved in planning processes are crucial when aiming for equity of supply 
with bike infrastructure. Results show that the trade-offs between travel time and desired properties of the route differ greatly across 
the subgroups of interest. It is seen that women, people cycling with children, and older adults have higher requirements regarding 
bike infrastructure, and in particular place higher value on lower speeds of motorized traffic than middle-aged males. If an alternative

is available, these groups pay for their needs with longer detours and consequently accept longer travel times compared to the average 
population. Strategies for a bike-friendly city should therefore contain concepts of lower speed limits and generally lower speeds in 
cities. Results show that specific subgroups benefit more from a bike-friendly redesign than the population as a whole. This applies to 
each of the user groups under consideration: women, people with children, and older adults.
